History of women in programming. Today programming is very male-dominated, and wild theories that women aren't as adept at math and science fly rampant. Clearly this didn't use to be the case. We'll talk about some women who pioneered the industry.

History of Women in Programming

  • Ada Lovelace
  • Grace Hopper
  • Erna Schnieder Hoover

Arguments still used today proclaim that more women aren't in tech because they don't excel at math and logic. Don't believe me? Spend sometime reading the comments on any article discussion gender imbalance in tech. I'm going to share with you the stories of a couple women who helped pioneer computer science the next time you see that argument used.

Ada Lovelace

“The more I study, the more insatiable do I feel my genius for it to be.”

Though computers didn't technically exist at the time, Ada Lovelace is credited with being a pioneer of computer science. She wrote the first algorithm in history, which theoretically would calculate numbers on the Analytical Engine. Ada described how to use punch cards to enter number. She was a revoluntionary thought leader with the foresight of what computers COULD do, and understanding of the possibilities that entailed. Her predictions indcludes the use of computers to create music and graphics, and have scientific uses.

Grace Hopper

“The most dangerous phrase in the language is, "We've always done it this way.”

There's a conference named after her for good reason. Grace Hopper was a Vassar College graduate with a BA in mathematics and physics, and upon entering the Navy became the 3rd person to program the Mark 1 computer. Working with computers, Grace theorized more people(read: non-mathmeticians) would be able to use them if they were able to be interacted with an a more humanized, readable way. She created what may be considered the first compiler: FLOW-MATIC, intended to complete tasks like billing and payroll based on english commands. She continued to advocate for human readable programming languages and was paramount in creating validation tools and internation standards for programming languages.

It was hard picking a quote from her, she was a serious badass and said a lot of things that are 100% relevant today

Erna Schnieder Hoover

“I designed the executive program for handling situations when there are too many calls, to keep it operating efficiently without hanging up on itself. Basically it was designed to keep the machine from throwing up its hands and going berserk.”

You may not have heard of Erna Schnieder Hoover, but you may as well call her the mother of telecommunications. Erna was a mathmetician, who when working at Bell Labs, invented the stored program control system, which was one of the first software patents ever recieved.

In the 1950's, when you went to make a phone call, an operator would ask you what number you could like to call, and manually connect you using a switchboard and array of cords, plugs, and switches. Large numbers of incomming calls would overload the switchboards being manually answered by phone operators.

Erna's invention was a computer system that monitored incoming call rates and automatically adjusted call's acceptance rate, largely eliminating overloading problems. This was the first of many computer programs Erna created to improve and maintain the quality of telecom services Bell provided.

Interesting note, Erna's husband, Charles Wilson Hoover, Jr., was extremely supportive of her career, though that was the opposite of desired wife behavior in the 1950's.

What Happened?

So we've highlighten some women rocking out in tech well into the 1950's. Computer science programs started popping up in university all over the country, including Columbia, Cambridge, and Harvard. And women are enrolling in them.

Then, in 1984 we seea steep decline in women enrolling in computer science. What the heck happened?

Computers for homes were produced ... and only marketed to boys

In the 1990s, Jane Margolis, a researcher, interviewed hundreds of computer science students at Carnegie Mellon University, one of the first universities to implement a computer science program. The research showed that families were much more likely to buy computers for boys than for girls — even when their girls were really interested in computers.

Why was this a problem? Because as personal computers became more common, computer science professors increasingly assumed that their students had grown up playing with computers at home, and suddenly women who didn't grow up playing on computers were expected to know things that the boys who were given computers did.

The Trouble With Bright Girls

“Bright girls believe that their abilities are innate and unchangeable, while bright boys believe that they can develop ability through effort and practice.”

https://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/the-science-success/201101/the-trouble-bright-girls

One of the best articles I've ever read is called "The Trouble with Bright Girls". The article discusses the research of psychologist Carol Dweck, who investigated how bright 5th grade boys and girls handled difficult and confusing new material. The results were troubling - the higher the girl's IQ, the more quickly she gave up, where as boys were energized by the challenge and would increase their efforts.

Bright girls believe that their abilities are innate and unchangeable, while bright boys believe that they can develop ability through effort and practice.

The likely cause is the way they have been spoken to as children. Girls mature more quickly, are more discipline, and feedback praises them for being good, well-behaved, pretty, intelligent. Boys, who are often more rambunctious are encouraged to "try harder", instead of being praised for already attained skills.

This feedback loop is dangeruos, because girls believe their attributes they are praised for are innate, therefor unable to change. Boys, on the otherhand learn to simply work harder.

This article BLEW MY MIND. I was a bright girl. I was praised for my intelligence. I breezed through AP level courses, and wasn't challenged until I got to college. I began flunking econ, was constantly bawling on the phone to my mother about my own incompetence, unable to understand why I wasn't smart enough.

Technology is a very intelligent field, filled with very intelligent people. Except most of those people are men, and as a women who's been praised for intelligence instead of the ability to improve, it's easy to fall prey to the idea that we're not smart enough to be there.

Current programs solving the gender issue.


@sheplusplus

SHE++ is a social enterprise that empowers women to make their mark on the technology industry. We believe that when women are equally represented in technology development, we accelerate innovation for those who need it most.


@girldevelopit

Girl Develop It is a nonprofit organization that exists to provide affordable and judgment-free opportunities for women interested in learning web and software development. Through in-person classes and community support, Girl Develop It helps women of diverse backgrounds achieve their technology goals and build confidence in their careers and their every day lives.


@girlswhocode

Girls Who Code programs work to inspire, educate, and equip girls with the computing skills to pursue 21st century opportunities.


@blackgirlscode

Black Girls Code's vision is to increase the number of women of color in the digital space by empowering girls of color ages 7 to 17 to become innovators in STEM fields, leaders in their communities, and builders of their own futures through exposure to computer science and technology.


@Hackbright

Hackbright Academy is the leading software engineering school for women founded in San Francisco in 2012. The academy graduates more female engineers than Stanford and UC Berkeley each year.


@pyladies

Pyladies is an international mentorship group with a focus on helping more women become active participants and leaders in the Python open-source community. Our mission is to promote, educate and advance a diverse Python community through outreach, education, conferences, events and social gatherings.


@ncwit

National Center for Women & Information Technology (NCWIT) is a non-profit community of more than 600 universities, companies, non-profits, and government organizations nationwide working to increase women’s participation in computing and technology. NCWIT equips change leaders with resources for taking action in recruiting, retaining, and advancing women from K–12 and higher education through industry and entrepreneurial careers.


@anitaborg_org

The Grace Hopper Celebration of Women in Computing is the World's Largest Gathering of Women Technologists. It is produced by the Anita Borg Institute and presented in partnership with ACM.

These organizations are just some of the few aiming to improve diversity in tech. They all offer fantastic resources, statistics, and curriculum, have locations all of the US, and world, and are great starting points for girls or women wanting to get into tech. Many also offer structures to create local chapters to easily bring their programming to your city.

How to improve your workplace.

  • Have women in leadership roles
  • Develop a mentorship program
  • Create a diversity support group
  • Watch your 'wage gap'
  • Revise HR policy
  • Advocate for better maternity/paternity leave policies
  • Provide facilities for new mothers
  • Offer Flex Time
  • Give employees hours to donate towards community service

Salesforce.com CEO Marc Benioff has been headling lately with his announcement of personally reviewing employee salaries to ensure equal pay among his male and female employees. Watch your wage gap. Any report you see shows a disparity in pay between men and women, sometimes as much as 30%. It's a problem that is slowly being resolved, now that attention has been drawn to it, so we're on the right track.

Revise your HR policy

Have women in leadership roles

Women in leadership roles is a no-brainer. However, do not interprut this as put "any women in a leadership role". This will do more harm than good. Instead pay attention to women who may have leadership potential but don't bring it to your attention in the ways you'd expect. Men are more likely to boast their achievements that make them promotion-worthy, and women are more likely to be quiet and humble in regards to theirs. I'll talk about the book Lean In later, but it explains this phenomenon at great length.

Develop a mentorship program

stats here

Creating a mentorship program can be huge. Women mentors are great, as young women can related to them on certain issues, like balancing a family. However, men are just as capable of giving great mentorship.

Create a diversity support group

stats here

Create a diversity support group. It could be a weekly luncheon, it could be a career-focused book dicussion group, or something much more informal. Until numbers skew more evenly in tech companies these groups provide solidarity for women.
